Introduction

This post continues the series looking at IPCC sectors in order of decreasing contributions to Canada's 2005 baseline year. The previous (2nd) post looked at Forest Land and Harvested Wood Products This one looks at fugitive emissions, especially of natural gas. Fugitive emissions of natural gas may admittedly sound like a natural and healthy bodily function (ha ha, ... anyone? sigh) but for Canada's GHG inventory, it a consequence of oil and gas extraction, processing, and distribution. Chemically, natural gas is mostly methane. Depending on exactly what geology it's sourced from, it is at least 60-70% methane at source, and once it has been processed to a marketable level of purity, it's at least 95% methane. The Canadian UOG sector assets and operations are vast. The 2011 emissions inventory included over 300 000 capable oil and gas wells, 14 100 batteries producing gas into more than 5000 gathering systems delivering to almost 750 gas plants, and 24 000 oil batteries delivering to 150 tank terminals, all interconnected by tens of thousands of kilometres of pipeline carrying hydrocarbons from wells to batteries to plants and ultimately markets (2005 NIR, pg 98). Across the oil and gas sector, methane escapes into the atmosphere for a variety of reasons (some accidental, some deliberate, some unavoidable), and methane is a much stronger greenhouse gas than carbon dioxide (see previous post on global warming potentials and CO2e).

Fugitive methane emissions from the oil and gas sector were about 70 MtCO2e in 2005, increased to a peak of 75 MtCO2e in 2014, and have since dropped to 40 MtCO2e even as oil and gas production has increased. That's about 50-10% of Canada's national emissions, and they are almost all from Alberta and Saskatchewan. Alberta set a target to reduce annual emissions by 45% relative to 2015, by the year 2025, and facility operators in Alberta have hit that target.

The determination of exactly how much methane is leaking from oil and gas operations, and from which specific sources, has been a scientific and operational challenge. Even a recent study by Matthew R. Johnson and colleagues (Johnson et al. 2017, Comparisons of Airborne Measurements and Inventory Estimates of Methane Emissions in the Alberta Upstream Oil and Gas Sector), which commissioned a plane with a spectrometer to fly over gas-producing and gas-processing regions and measure methane plumes, concluded that 2.5 times more methane was leaking than the best government estimates of the day. The 2025 NIR (overview pdf, see esp section 3.3.2.2) draws on several types of data to estimate fugitive emissions of natural gas: direct estimation by facilities to the extent that they have information regarding certain emission events, modelling to infer emissions from the remaining activities and facilities, atmospheric measurements of the type introduced by Johnson et al. 2017 linked above, and interpolation and extrapolation to re-estimate the emissions for certain historical years based on an evolving understanding of the drivers of fugitive methane.

Drivers of Fugitive Vented Emissions of Natural Gas

From an IPCC accounting persepctive, the sector we're talking about is subset of Common Reporting Table category 1.B.2. (Fugitive Emissions from the Oil and Gas Sector) relating specifically to vented natural gas, which is natural gas that has been released directly into the atmosphere. The sector covers emissions from many sources, which contributes to the difficulty of estimation and management of the emissions. Despite the challenges of tracking how much methane is escaping from each of these types of source, the set of which sources are responsible for fugitive methane emissions is well understood.

  • Upstream Oil and Gas operations produce fugitive, vented emissions in many ways:
    • drilling, servicing, and associated testing
    • natural gas production (sometimes in combination with oil), due to accidental and deliberate emissions from equipment in normal operation, maintenance, and failure recovery scenarios
    • oil production, due to solution gas that evaporates from oil as it flows from a high-pressure deposit into a well, and casing gas that evaporates for the same reason after the point at which oil has entered a pipe
    • natural gas processing and transmission, due to equipment leaks
    • liquid product transfer, due to leaks and venting
    • accidents and equipment failures across all segments of the upstream oil and gas industry
    • surface casing vent flow and gas migration, due to e.g. casing leaks or gas-bearing zones that have been penetrated but are not managed with production equipment.
  • Bitumen extraction from oil sands produces fugitive vented emissions due to:
    • usual process losses in the use of natural gas to produce the hydrogen required for processing
    • methane released from open mining surface
    • methane released by bacteria from tailings and settling ponds.
  • Abandoned oil and gas wells should be plugged with cement, but sometimes are not.
  • Downstream (aka post-production) oil and gas operations and equipment can leak at all stages of processing and distribution, both before and after metering.
This set of sources has been condensed as the Critical Success Factors of newly-created IPCC Sectors page on "Venting (Fugitive Sources / Oil and Natural Gas)".

Estimating Fugitive Vented Emissions of Natural Gas

The reference for how to estimate the NIR's fugitive vented emissions of natural gas is the ECCC's Methodologies regarding Fugitive Emissions. For lack of a single national data set other than the NIR itself, the planzero Vented Natural Gas (VNG) estimator was built from provincial sources. Only three provinces contributed significantly to the VNG total: Alberta, Saskatchewan, and British Columbia.

Alberta: ST60B and OneStop

The first data set used to build the planzero VNG estimator was Statistical Report 60 B from Alberta's Energy Regulator (AER-ST60B), the "Upstream Petroleum Industry Emissions Report" for year ending Dec 31, 2024 (pdf). AER-ST60B includes a spreadsheet with data from the Petrinex and OneStop reporting systems regarding all reported venting emissions from years 2020-2024 on a facility by facility basis. In theory, Petrinex vent gas volumes include both routine and non-routine vent incidents, and OneStop includes fewer incidents, but with more detail. In practice, the text of ST60B observes (and laments) that the sum of OneStop-reported emissions is numerically larger than the sum of Petrinex-reported emissions by about 8%. The planzero VNG estimator developed for this post used the OneStop emissions.

The Reason: Before 2020, gas used to power pneumatic devices was often reported as "Fuel" because it was being "used." The Change: Regulators (AER, BCER, SK) updated their definitions to align with the NIR. Now, any gas that eventually enters the atmosphere must be reported as "Vent."
