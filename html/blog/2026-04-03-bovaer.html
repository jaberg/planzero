Introduction

This post represents a break from the series replicating 2025 National Inventory Report sectoral totals, to do some relatively simple modelling, of what the impact might be if beef and dairy farmers were to gradually transition to administering Bovaer. Bovaer is a brand name for a feed additive for cattle and sheep, also known as 3-Nitrooxypropanol, also known as 3-NOP. It is a synthetic organic compound that, when added to ruminant livestock feed in the correct manner, has the effect of inhibiting methane production in their first stomach. Bovaer is approved for use in Canada. The modelling presented in this post codifies assumptions about what Bovaer does, how much it costs, which kinds of cattle would consume it, how long it would take farmers to adopt it (and how many might might never adopt it), how much it might cost to verify that farmers that were supposed to be administering it really are doing that, and what fraction of purchased Bovaer is actually making it into cattle as intended.

This post also introduce some new terminology (such as strategy, barrier, critical success factor, model, and scenario) and visualizations based on this terminology. These terms are explained in more detail in a follow-up post ("A glossary of terms ...") but the common definitions are sufficient for this post to make sense without the more precise definitions in the follow-up. This post and the follow-up glossary post were actually drafted together, and the follow-up makes heavy use of this post as its illustrative running example. The visualizations can be found within pages under the Strategies and Models tabs above.

This post does not establish a position on the question of whether Canada should fund Bovaer for a number of reasons, foremost among them being (1) that Bovaer is the only strategy implemented in the PlanZero model at this time so there's no less-expensive or more-expensive alternatives with which to compare, and (2) that anyway the model makes no attempt to anticipate government decision-making. What the post does do, is calculate that Bovaer usage represents a strategy that costs about $210/t{{CO2e|safe}} according to the modelling assumptions below.

The post is organized as follows:

  • The Bovaer Adoption Strategy: what's the idea here?
  • Relevant Critical Success Factors: what factors drive enteric fermentation emissions, and how does the strategy aim to help?
  • Barriers to Adoption, Limits to Success: what intermediate things relate our strategy to the critical success factors?
  • Simulating the Model: when we simulate a model with these assumptions, what happens?

The Bovaer Adoption Strategy

The motivating strategy in this post is straightforward: pay farmers to adopt Bovaer. The model is based on an average farm subsidy of $5000/year to compel farmers to feed their cattle Bovaer (based on average farm size of 160 cattle).

Relevant Critical Success Factors

The emissions calculation for enteric emissions (see this earlier post approximating the enteric fermentation emissions) can be approximated as the product of two terms (ignoring ruminants other than cattle): number of cattle, and average methane emitted per head of cattle. These two terms suggest two critical success factors to reducing enteric fermentation emissions:

  • Minimize the number of cattle.
  • Minimize the average amount of methane emitted per head of cattle.
Minimizing either or both of these terms would, mathematically, reduce enteric fermentation emissions. Their product will not be zero unless one of them is, itself, zero.

The strategy of administering Bovaer is aimed solely at the second term. Bovaer reduces the methane emitted by a cow by different amounts depending on the cow's diet, breed and milk production. The manufacturer estimates that methane emissions are reduced by 30% for dairy cows, and 45% for beef cows (link).

Barriers to Adoption, Limits to Success

The model implemented in PlanZero for Bovaer adoption is simple, but it is not so simple that if the government just approves an average of $5000/year per farmer, it will see an immediate 30-45% reduction in methane emissions from enteric fermentation. The PlanZero model for Bovaer adoption is structured as three barriers:
BarrierDescriptionSector
Bovaer Adoption Limit Assume Bovaer will only be adopted by, at most, 5.0 percent / year of cattle operations, up to a maximum of 99% (the remainder of which are organic farms, which cannot administer Bovaer). Enteric Fermentation
Bovine Population Model cattle population, emission of methane (factoring in Bovaer), and the cost of administered Bovaer. Cattle population is modelled as fixed after 2026. Enteric Fermentation, Other Product Manufacture and Use
Bovaer Monitoring Assume administering and monitoring costs 1000.0 CAD / farm / year for paperwork, 3000.0 CAD / farm / year for on-site inspection, and 5000.0 CAD / farm / year in farm subsidies Enteric Fermentation

The "Bovaer Adoption Limit" barrier defines the maximum fraction of cattle that may be on Bovaer on a given year as being at most 5% more than the fraction of cattle actually on Bovaer in the previous year. Further, it assumes that organic farmers (estimated to be 1% of farmers) will never adopt Bovaer, because Bovaer is incompatible with the production of organic milk and beef.

The "Bovine Population" barrier assumes that the national herd size and composition will remain constant from 2026 onward, and that each year the various types of cattle will produce methane as they to today if they are not on Bovaer, and at reduced rates if they are on Bovaer. Cattle on Bovaer are modelled as producing 30% less if they are dairy cows, 45% less if they are beef cows, and similar numbers that I guessed if they are other kinds of cattle (I could not find data).

The "Bovine Population" barrier also implements the assumption that Bovaer will cost money. It estimates the cost of the Bovaer required to be from $0.20/day for calves up to $0.50/day for adults. It also estimates the {{CO2e|safe}} required to produce the Bovaer; I couldn't find a reference for this number, so the model assumes it is 5% of the greenhouse gas reduction delivered by the Bovaer usage. This {{CO2e|safe}} is attributed to the IPCC Sector "Industrial / Other Product Manufacture and Use" as {{CO2|safe}} until I have a better model of how Bovaer is produced.

The "Bovaer Monitoring" barrier incorporates assumptions regarding how much it is expected to cost to monitor and verify that Bovaer was actually administered as it should be. The model assumes $1000 / farm /year for administrative overhead, and $3000 / farm / year for onsite inspection. Confidence intervals are not yet supported in PlanZero, but if they were, I would use large ones around these figures as I did not find references (github issue here). This barrier assumes a constant estimate of 160 cattle / farm on average, rather than the actual farm data available from Statistics Canada which has hovered around 160 in recent years.

Simulating the Model

PlanZero can run as a simulator, and roll out the year-by-year consequences of the modelling assumptions above. Note that the model implicitly assumes that it is complete, and that nothing else changes in the National Greenhouse Gas Inventory after 2026 except the gradual adoption of Bovaer. Assuming farmers actually go for the $5000/year deal they're offered, the following figure is what the inventory would look like out to 2050 and beyond.

For most sectors, the figure shows historical actuals until 2023, followed by constant projections. For the "Enteric Fermentation" sector (scaling scenario), we see a steady reduction in emissions as our simulated farmers adopt Bovaer at 5% / year. At the same time, we see a steady (although smaller) rise in emissions in the "Other Product Manufacture and Use" sector. After all eligible farms have adopted Bovaer, annual emissions are reduced by 9.5 Mt {{CO2e|safe}}. The magnitude of subsidy required is shown in dark red, and plotted against the right vertical axis in billions of dollars. As farmers adopt Bovaer it rises to a total just over 2.0 billion dollars per year. Since Bovaer usage and cost is proportional and synchronous, the cost of emissions reduction from using Bovaer can be computed directly by dividing these two numbers: 2.0 billion dollars / 9.5 Mt {{CO2e|safe}} equates to $210.45 / t{{CO2e|safe}}.

The impact of the strategy on the overall national picture is significant, but still relatively small (about 1.3%). Ablative analysis (looking at the results with and without the strategy) can visualize the effects of a single strategy more clearly. Conclusion

With this post, PlanZero now includes a strategy for emission reduction (ScaleBovaer), and a model of the impact of that strategy. In this model, the strategy of paying farmers an average of $5000 / year to administer Bovaer is estimated to deliver up to 9.5Mt {{CO2e|safe}}/year of emissions reductions by 2050 at a cost of about $210/t{{CO2e|safe}}.

When I think of these numbers — 9.5Mt {{CO2e|safe}}/year by 2050 for $210/t{{CO2e|safe}} — I feel like a blind man first setting foot on an unexplored world of possible futures. I am surrounded by potential issues and unknowns; now what? I know others have done this sort of modelling before, so perhaps I should imagine instead that I've blind-folded myself and landed on a crowded beach, but if the reader will indulge me, I will articulate the potential issues and unknowns that come to my mind, as many possible directions for next steps:

  • How does this estimate compare with conventional wisdom on the price of Bovaer in Canada?
  • What other technologies are on the table at a hypothetical budget of $210/t?
  • Could e.g. drones or flyovers monitor enteric emissions at lower cost? How much would the cost of this strategy need to come down for it to be viable?
  • I think people ultimately want to hear positions on matters of debate, not simulation results and model predictions. How might PlanZero take positions while remaining rooted in data and open source modelling?
  • Maybe a few more strategy models are in order before focusing so much on emission avoidance and carbon capture. The main high-impact strategies in the public eye, and identified in the modelling posts so far are:
    • Solar, wind, tidal, geothermal, and/or nuclear energy for grid-scale power generation
    • EVs for light-duty vehicles
    • Heat pumps for residential and commercial/institutional heating
    • Carbon Capture and [Utilization] and Storage
  • How can I relate PlanZero to other modelling efforts more generally? (see gh issue re: Bibliography)
  • I can't always write one post at a time. There was too much information in the first version of this post, and it was a jumble. I really like the linearity and typical scope of the posts so far, how can I work my way through big chunks when required? (Edit: this has been addressed for now through introducion of "Draft Status" as described in subsequent post "About this project..." April 12, 2026.)
  • If the strategy analysis in this post is any indication, modelling many strategies across many sectors is going to get complicated. How should this complexity be managed in terms of communication, and in terms of implementation? I've used the terminology of critical success factor, barrier, strategy, model, and scenario in this post; time will tell how much it helps. (Edit: "A glossary of terms...", April 19, 2026 elaborates on the definitions and rationale of these terms in PlanZero.)
  • Probabilities and uncertainty is critical to this sort of modelling, and is completely absent from all PlanZero posts to date, including this one.

So what's actually next? The nearest-term deliverable that I'm aiming to produce is a forecast of the National Greenhouse Gas Inventory, specifically one that is focused on the next few years ahead of the most-recent National Inventory Report. This deliverable requires, at minimum, that PlanZero implement (1) at least one model of what the inventory might look like, other than the flat-lines-to-the-right model shown above in this post, (2) at least one model-comparison metric that says which is better, between the new model and the flat-line model, and (3) some confidence intervals. The next two posts after this one take a step back from modelling to reflect on my development process and the terms used to describe what exactly is a model in PlanZero. After those, I'll return to modelling, and introduce a generic baseline statistical model for national inventory forecasting that will predict something, and force some discussion of probabilities and uncertainty.

Until then,

- James Bergstra
