Light-Duty Gasoline Truck Emissions

This is the sixth post in the sector-by-sector series replicating Canada's National Greenhouse Gas Inventory Report (NIR). It introduces PlanZero's first estimator for mobile combustion, which was chosen to be Energy / Transport / Road Transportation / Light-Duty Gasoline Truck. This post brings an IPCC-Sector summary page, updated critical success factors and barriers, and some discussion of the granular data on this sector that's available from the Natural Resources Canada (NRCan). The only broad strategy for significant carbon reduction in this sector is a transition to EVs.

So-called "Light-Duty Gasoline Trucks" in the context of the NIR refers to vehicles such as

  • Pickup Trucks: (e.g., Ford F-150, Chevy Silverado)
  • SUVs / Crossovers: (e.g., Ford Explorer, Toyota RAV4, Honda CR-V)
  • Commercial Vans: (e.g., Ford Transit Connect)
  • Minivans: (e.g., Dodge Caravan, Honda Odyssey)
These are primarily road vehicles with a gross vehicle weight rating (GVWR) of up to 3960 kg (8500 lbs), designed with greater off-road or cargo capability relative to cars. Data from National Resources Canada, which publishes the National Energy Use Database (NEUD) (and a great mini-site overview of drivers and solutions for this sector) highlight that although engine efficiency has improved, emissions in this sector are rising because more people are using these larger vehicles instead of smaller cars.

The emissions from gasoline-burning engines are an inevitable consequence of the combustion process that provides propulsion. What may be surprising is how much {{CO2|safe}} is emitted. To understand why, consider that gasoline is a liquid hydrocarbon fuel, which means it is almost entirely made up of hydrogen and carbon atoms, even if those atoms can be found in many many molecular configurations. Octane for example, has 8 carbon atoms and 18 hydrogen atoms; heptane has 7 and 16 respectively; and benzene has 6 of each. Gasoline combusts at sufficiently high pressure and temperature in the presence of oxygen, and creates two exhaust products: carbon dioxide ({{CO2|safe}}) and water vapour. The chemical reaction for the combustion of octane, for example, looks like this:

{{blog.terms.gas_combustion|safe}}

Notice that twenty five oxygen molecules are required to combust 2 octane molecules. Although gasoline is a somewhat variable mixture of octane and other molecules, typically about 2.3 to 2.65 kg of pure oxygen is required to combust a litre of the stuff. This is equivalent to roughly 9,000 liters of air at standard temperature and pressure. I bring this up, because it is a way to appreciate why emissions from gasoline combustion are so much more than the amount of fuel that goes into the vehicle. Most of the mass of the {{CO2|safe}} emissions from combustion are from the oxygen, not the gasoline. The emission factor for gasoline used in the NIR is 2.307 kg {{CO2|safe}} / litre (NIR Annex 6: Emission Factors). Burning a standard tank of gas in Canada, say 65 litres, produces, on average, about 150 kg of {{CO2|safe}} emissions.

Estimating Residential Stationary Combustion Emissions

To replicate and break down emissions for this sector I used data related to transportation from the per-province "comprehensive tables" of NRCan's National Energy Use Database (NEUD). This data set refers to energy use by various "transportation modes", including "Passenger Light Trucks" and "Freight Light Trucks". The quality-of-fit of this data to the National Inventory Report target is nearly perfect for some years (e.g. 2011-2014), low for some years (e.g. 2006-2010) and high for other years (2016-2023). The estimator is at least slightly wrong because it isn't supposed to include the small number of diesel-powered light truck vehicles, but it currently does. If we imagine removing the contributions from diesel vehicles from the PlanZero estimator, then the estimator would be lower, and it would be more-wrong for years 2006 to 2010. I do not know what might be added in to this estimator to put things right for those years. Still, the estimator is relatively accurate, at least by the standards of this PlanZero first pass.

Critical Success Factors

The arithmetic of emissions in this sector is such some combination of the following is required:

  1. Reduce number of gasoline vehicles of this class
  2. Reduce distance travelled by this class of vehicle under gasoline power
  3. Improve gasoline engine efficiency

Barriers

Before we get into barriers, it must be stated that the obvious and preeminent strategy for emissions reduction in this sector is a transition to EVs.

  • Comfort: People like to drive, and they like to drive at high speeds, in relative privacy inside large, spacious personal vehicles. This requires additional energy.
  • Identity: Brand marketing plays a strong role in car puchasing decisions. Vehicle choices have become an integral part of many people's professional and personal image.
  • Longevity: Vehicles sales in this class account for about 10% of the number of vehicle registrations. It could take decades to change the technology mix within this sector.
  • EV Range: Battery power density is such that EVs cannot yet match the range that's possible using a large gasoline tank. Charging a battery isn't as convenient as refilling a tank.
  • EV Sticker Price: Non-hybrid internal combustion vehicles are cheaper than hybrid and EV variants, similar models.
  • EV Cost Complexity: The total cost of ownership comparison for EVs and PHEVs is less than ICE vehicles in many use cases, but the calculation depends on several factors, not all of which are top-of-mind or even well-known to buyers.
  • EV Pace of Deprecation: The relatively rapidly evolving technology in EVs may discourage some buyers (holding out for e.g. better range at lower cost next year), and some sellers (who wish their mid-life EVs commanded higher resale prices).
  • EV Range Anxiety: Managing a battery charge level is different from keeping a gasoline tank full. In some cases it's easier, but in some cases it's more difficult. EV charging infrastructure lags gasoline refuelling infrastructure by most of a century; charging an EV on the road can be an inconvenience, or a real challenge.
  • Lithium batteries degrade in cold temperatures: Cold temperatures degrade lithium-ion battery performance more than gasoline engine performance. Sodium ion batteries are more robust to cold, may help in future designs.
  • Gasoline engine efficiency is hard to improve: Gasoline engines have already been refined over almost a century of intense engineering. Higher fuel efficiency is possible, but such engines are expensive relative to their fuel savings.

Strategies

The overarching strategy for emissions reduction in this sector is a transition to EVs. EV makers appear to be able to provide drop-in replacement vehicles for the gasoline vehicles that dominate this sector today. Many operational light-duty trucks could be replaced with EVs (battery electric or plug-in hybrid) of today's price and capability when they're due for replacement, in such a way that the replacements would provide equivalent or better functionality at a lower cost of ownership. The strategies required to eliminate direct emissions from light-duty trucks are incentives that encourage individuals and fleet managers to buy the EVs already for sale.

That said, there are operational roles that EVs still struggle with: for vehicles that aren't driven enough, an EV won't save enough on fuel to achieve a lower cost of ownership; and for vehicles that are driven too much; an EV risks inconveniencing its drivers by needing to be charged (slowly) while en-route rather than while parked overnight. Research in better batteries may address these challenges, although more radical ideas have been proposed, such as providing inductive charging capabilities underneath roadways, or charging lines that hang down from overhead. Such radical proposals would probably never be justified for only light-duty passenger vehicles, but radical solutions might be required to eliminate emissions from heavy-duty trucks, for which EV capability readiness lags significantly behind that of light-duty truck EVs. If such systems are developed for heavy-duty trucks, they may be useful to light-duty ones as well.

Conclusion

The IPCC category of Light-Duty Gasoline Trucks is a big one for Canada because Canadians drive a lot, and they like to drive SUVs and pickups. Historically, these vehicles have been almost universally gasoline-powered. More recently, plug-in hybrids and purely battery-electric vehicles have been brought to market in this category. I think it's likely that these EVs can and will replace the gasoline models over time, although I expect consumers will continue to push for better power-to-weight batteries than the ones in today's new vehicles.

This post has introduced a relatively accurate estimator of emissions based on data from Natural Resources Canada's National Energy Use Database. The Transport / Road Transport / Light-Duty Gasoline Trucks page has been refreshed to feature this estimator, as well as updated critical success factors, barriers to those success factors, and strategies to address those factors and barriers. It's a large emission source, and I believe that, unlike residential heating (use heatpumps) and gasoline vehicles (use EVs), the leading strategies for what to do about it are much less far along.
