Introduction

The previous post introduced greenhouse gases and radiative forcing, which is the mechanism by which greenhouse gases increase the heat retained by the planetary system of the earth. This post introduces Canada’s net-zero commitments: the signing of the UN Paris Accords (developed with input from the International Panel on Climate Change, IPCC), and the passing of the Canadian Net-Zero Emissions Accountability Act (CNZEAA). Discussion of the CNZEAA motivates an upgrade to the IPCC sector emissions figure from this blog's first post: namely adding Canada's Net-Zero commitments and targets under the CNZEAA, and visualizing the sink sectors in addition to the source sectors.

Taking a step back, I believe the structure of Canada's net-zero commitments is important to write about because (1) I would like to use PlanZero to evaluate business ideas, and businesses address private problems, and (2) the roll-up of economic activity into national GHG emissions appears to mirror (in reverse) the breakdown of public problems into private ones. By replicating here in PlanZero at least some of the computation of the national [GHG] inventory, I hope to systematically break down the public problem of GHG emissions into the many contributing factors from across the economy, in a way that can be used to evaluate business ideas from an emissions-impact perspective. This post introduces Canada's targets, and the framework in which they are defined. In doing so, it sets up a series of follow-on posts about replicating (if sometimes approximating) the sector-by-IPCC-sector totals that, together, make up Canada's national greenhouse gas inventory.

Paris Accords

Some of Canada’s indigenous peoples practiced a “seven-generations” doctrine, which emphasized the consideration of future generations in decision-making. However, Canada’s federal constitution, laws and governance mechanisms have historically not included mechanisms or principles to look and plan far-enough ahead to consider the long-term negative impacts of greenhouse gas emissions. The starting point for PlanZero’s modelling of Canada’s current federal commitments to net-zero greenhouse gas emissions is Canada's signing of the Paris Agreement in 2016, as part of the United Nations Framework Convention on Climate Change (UNFCCC).

From wikipedia: The Paris Agreement (also called the Paris Accords or Paris Climate Accords) is an international treaty on climate change that was signed in 2016. The treaty covers climate change mitigation, adaptation, and finance. The Paris Agreement was negotiated by 196 parties at the 2015 United Nations Climate Change Conference near Paris, France. The Paris Agreement has a long-term temperature goal which is to keep the rise in global surface temperature to well below 2 °C (3.6 °F) above pre-industrial levels. The treaty also states that preferably the limit of the increase should only be 1.5 °C (2.7 °F). These limits are defined as averages of the global temperature as measured over many years. The lower the temperature increase, the smaller the expected effects of climate change. To achieve this temperature goal, greenhouse gas emissions should be reduced as soon as, and by as much as, possible. They should even reach net zero by the middle of the 21st century. To stay below 1.5 °C of global warming, emissions need to be cut by roughly 50% by 2030.

Under the Paris Agreement, each country must determine its own emissions targets (exceeding its previous targets), plan, and regularly report on its greenhouse gas (GHG) contributions. Countries are not legally obligated by the Paris Accord to set particular targets, nor to hit those targets, but countries are obliged to have targets on emissions as measured in particular ways, to communicate those targets, and then to report on their progress using standard methodology. This activity is the foundation of what might colloquially be called a name-and-shame system, or what has semi-officially been called a “name and encourage” system. The Paris Accords exist within a realm of international relations that already includes a range of mechanisms for such encouragement to be applied between countries. The Paris Agreement creates transparency so that other kinds of political and economic mechanisms can exert aligned encouragement toward the global net-zero goals of the UNFCCC.

Canadian Net-Zero Emissions Accountability Act

Canada passed the Canadian Net Zero Emissions Accountability Act (CNZEAA, 2021) in order to implement the changes required to meet its responsibilities under the Paris Accords. The implications of the CNZEAA are far-reaching. For a few introductions to the act, see e.g. the full text, the overview maintained by the civil service, and the Honourable Senator Rosa Galvez’s “Short Guide to the Canadian Net-Zero Emissions Accountability Act”).

The Paris Accords are primarily about standardizing how countries report emissions and plans. The CNZEAA makes Canada’s Ministry of the Environment and Climate Change (ECCC) primarily accountable for planning and reporting on Canada’s behalf. The ECCC reports on both its own work and the work of many other areas of the public and private sectors. The CNZEAA directly and indirectly added responsibilities across many areas of federal and provincial government. For a few examples:

Ministry of the Environment and Climate Change (ECCC)Ultimately accountable for reporting ot the UNFCCC
Ministry of Natural Resources (NRCan)Reporting and planning in some sectors, especially energy, industrial, and land use
Statistics Canada (StatsCan)Collecting and reporting many economic statistics that supporting emissions estimates
Ministry of FinanceReporting and planning for government agencies' internal operations and the same for crown corporations
Provinces and TerritoriesJurisdictional reporting and planning, especially natural resources, energy, land use, buildings, transportation
Net-Zero Advisory BodyNewly-created scientific advisory group created by CNZEAA

The CNZEAA required the ECCC to publish a 2030 emissions reduction plan by 2023, and to update it with progress reports in 2025 and 2027, leading up to a major replanning iteration in 2029-2030. The ECCC has done so: it maintains 2030 targets via the ECCC website, and last year published the 2025 progress report. The 2030 plan is substantial, and will drive many future blog-updates to PlanZero.

ECCC's Latest National GHG Targets and LULUCF

The ECCC has set national targets for the years 2030, 2035, and 2050:

  • 2030: 40% below 2005 levels
  • 2035: 40-45% below 2005 levels
  • 2050: 100% below 2005 levels
As of this blog post, PlanZero includes these federal targets as a curve overlayed on the IPCC Sectors page.

Also overlayed now are two other curves: the total net emissions with and without the Land Use, Land Use Change, and Forestry (LULUCF) sectors. Two net emissions totals (with and without LULUCF) are plotted because LULUCF sector presents complications and is excluded from totals in some circumstances. Canada's National Inventory is prepared by the ECCC for the United Nations (compiled strictly according to the IPCC guidelines), and the IPCC requires that LULUCF be included as an Annex. I expect LULUCF is treated differently for at least the following reasons:

  • Few nations have large forested areas.
  • It is not currently possible to characterize LULUCF impacts with as much precision as other sectors.
  • Natural randomness plays a significant role in annual emission events relating to land.
  • The amplitude of specific emission events from e.g. forest wildfires can be large relative to other sectors.
  • Forest wildfire emissions in particular are naturally cyclic and historically sustainable, unlike emissions from combustion of fossil carbon.
That said, Canada's vast land mass is a unique resource that can help achieve Canada's net-zero goal. For that reason (perhaps), Canada has adopted Net-Zero targets in terms of the total national emissions inclusive of LULUCF emissions. Still, in keeping with IPCC reporting guidelines, the "Total" in Canada's National Inventory Report is exclusive of LULUCF emissions.

Summary

The UNFCCC's Paris Accords, established in 2015, seem to be holding strong as an international agreement to (a) define greenhouse gases (GHGs), (b) recognize the role of anthropogenic greenhouse gas emissions in affecting the global climate, and (c) standardize methodology for collecting and reporting nation-by-nation data on global emissions. Canada signed the Paris Accords, and subsequently passed the Canadian Net-Zero Emissions Accountability Act (CNZEAA) in order to establish the programs and responsibilities across many areas of government, that ultimately allow the Ministry of Environment and Climate Change (ECCC) to fulfil Canada's reporting obligations to the International Panel on Climate Change (IPCC). Canada has set GHG emission targets in terms of total net IPCC sectoral emissions: 40% below the 2005 total by 2030, 45-50% below by 2035, and 100% below by 2050. PlanZero's IPCC Sectors page has been updated to show these targets.

Coming up over the next several blog-updates will be the development of an estimate of the national inventory on the basis of data from StatScan and NRCan. The aim is to connect emissions to the most detailed available data from Canada's economic activity and land use. The purpose of this PlanZero project is partly for me to learn about all of the sources of Canada's GHG emissions, but also, beyond that, to systematically break down the public problem of too-much-GHG-emissions into the many private problems and decisions about which e.g. StatsCan and NRCan collect survey data. I would like to use PlanZero to evaluate business ideas, and businesses address private problems. I plan to go through the IPCC Sectors in order of decreasing magnitude in the 2005 emissions baseline, making time-boxed efforts to estimate each one as well as possible. First up: Public Electricity and Heat.
